Meet Maria Santos, a Senior Python Developer who made an incredible journey from studying biology to becoming one of Manila's most respected Python developers. Her story is one of courage, persistence, and the power of community support in overcoming imposter syndrome.

Key Takeaways from Maria's Journey

  • Career transitions are possible at any stage of life
  • Community support is crucial for overcoming self-doubt
  • Your unique background brings valuable perspectives to tech
  • Imposter syndrome affects everyone, even senior developers

Full Transcript

Interviewer: Maria, thank you for joining us today. Can you start by telling us about your background before you got into programming?

Maria: Of course! I actually have a degree in Biology from UP Diliman. I was working in a research lab for about three years, doing mainly data analysis on environmental samples. I kept finding myself more excited about analyzing the data than collecting it, and that's when I realized I might be more suited for a different path.

Interviewer: That's fascinating. How did you make the transition from biology to programming?

Maria: It wasn't easy, I'll be honest. I started learning Python on my own because it was being used in the lab for some of our data analysis. I was amazed by how powerful it was ‚Äì I could automate tasks that were taking me hours to do manually. Python felt like magic at first. I remember the first time I wrote a script that processed hundreds of data files in seconds instead of the days it used to take me.

Interviewer: When did you decide to make programming your full-time career?

Maria: That decision took about a year of night classes and weekend coding. I was terrified, honestly. Here I was, a biology graduate trying to break into tech. The imposter syndrome was real ‚Äì I felt like everyone would immediately know I didn't belong. But I had built up a small portfolio of data analysis projects, and I decided to take the leap.

Interviewer: How did you find your first programming job?

Maria: I applied to dozens of junior developer positions. Most didn't even respond, which was discouraging. But eventually, I found a small startup that was looking for someone who could work with scientific data. Interviewer: How has the community helped you grow?

Maria: In so many ways! The technical workshops helped me learn new skills, but more importantly, seeing other women who had made similar career transitions gave me confidence. Interviewer: Any final thoughts for our listeners?

But that's actually normal ‚Äì technology changes so fast that we're all constantly learning. The key is to embrace that continuous learning and remember that your unique journey is what makes you valuable to any team.
